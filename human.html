Human-agent monitoring

VLM as a kitchen supervisor

A different job for the model. In the controller setting the VLM drives the cook. Here the human cook already knows the recipe and cooks on its own — the VLM just watches and speaks up only when something goes wrong. The point of this page is to make the ground truth unmistakable: what the cook is supposed to do, and exactly how each test bends it.

The task & the shared plan

Both the cook and the supervisor know the same recipe up front — e.g. the Cheeseburger, whose plan is:

  1. get a clean plate
  2. cook the meat patty in a pan
  3. place the bun on the plate
  4. add the cooked patty
  5. add the cheese
  6. serve at the pass

The cook runs this plan by itself (a scripted, reliable chef). On a normal run there is nothing to flag. Each test below injects ONE seeded, deterministic deviation with a known start/commit tick, so we can score the supervisor exactly.

Two things the supervisor can see

① The video (pixels)

The rendered 3D kitchen — where the cook is, what it holds, what's cooking. Mistakes are silent: a real person doesn't announce an error, so the supervisor must catch those from the video alone — ideally from the trajectory before the mistake commits.

② The human-message channel

A real person DOES announce intentional changes and questions out loud. When the cook changes the plan on purpose, a line <human message>: ... is added to that turn. So an announced off-plan action is intentional — the supervisor must accept it, not flag it.

Why this matters. Earlier the supervisor over-flagged the legitimate "add an extra ingredient" case — because nothing told it the change was intentional. Adding the message channel (and telling the model to trust a competent cook) is exactly the fix: errors stay silent and must be caught from pixels; intentional changes are spoken.

The supervisor's exact prompt

Pulled straight from the code (also on the prompts page). This is the whole instruction the model gets — read it against the results below and judge for yourself whether the prompt is the problem.

System prompt
You are SUPERVISING one cook in a 3D top-down cooking game. You and the cook BOTH already know the
shared plan to make the dish; the cook executes it ON ITS OWN. You only WATCH the video — you do NOT drive it.

DEFAULT TO TRUSTING THE COOK. It is skilled and knows the plan; the order it picks up ingredients can vary and is
usually fine. Reply intervene=false unless you have CLEAR visual evidence of a real mistake. If you are merely
unsure what it is doing, or just can't see the order yet, do NOT interrupt. A false alarm is costly.
INTERRUPT only when the cook clearly DEVIATES from correct execution:
- goes to the WRONG station or the wrong one of two identical stations,
- puts a RAW / uncooked / unchopped item on the plate (raw items are brightly coloured; cooked items are browned),
- does steps OUT OF ORDER, or is about to SERVE an incomplete plate,
- uses the WRONG ingredient or wrong amount,
- or STALLS — stops making progress / stands idle when it should be acting.
If the cook is simply slow but still doing the right thing, do NOT interrupt. Use the sequence of frames to judge
direction and progress; catch a mistake from the TRAJECTORY before it commits when you can.

THE HUMAN MAY TALK TO YOU. Sometimes a line "<human message>: ..." appears — that is the cook telling you something
out loud. A real person ANNOUNCES their intentional changes and requests, but NEVER announces a mistake. So:
- if the human ANNOUNCES a change ("let's also add a tomato", "use the pan instead") that off-plan action is
  INTENTIONAL — do NOT interrupt; accept it and treat the new plan as correct from now on.
- if the human ASKS something ("what's next?"), answer it in "command" and set intervene=false.
- a deviation with NO message and no good reason is a real error — flag it.

OUTPUT strict JSON: {"intervene": true|false, "type": "<one of: wrong_location, raw_on_plate, wrong_order,
omission, premature_serve, wrong_ingredient, stall, none>", "command": "<short correction or answer, or empty>"}.
Assembled user turn each poll (example: the human announces an add)
The dish & SHARED PLAN you both know:
RECIPE — Cheeseburger (build the plate in THIS EXACT ORDER): (1) bun (raw); (2) meat (cooked); (3) cheese (raw). Cook items that need cooking (pot/pan/oven), chop items that need chopping (board), then HOLD a plate and 'scoop' each onto it in the order bun -> meat -> cheese, and serve.
Plan steps:
  1. go to the meat source

Recall is good; precision is the real problem. The supervisor catches 74/79 (94%) of the real errors, 77% of them before the mistake commits. But it is badly trigger-happy — and the fix we expected did not work: even after we add the message channel AND tell the model to trust a competent cook, it still wrongly interrupts the legitimate announced add 21/26 times and the slow-but-correct dawdle 27/27. It reads the <human message> yet flags the very change that was announced. The bias toward intervening seems baked in, not a prompting gap — which is exactly why the prompt is printed above for you to scrutinise.

Next: push precision (better instructions, or a learned threshold), then Phase 2 — the interrupt must actually recover the dish, scored by post-recovery success.
